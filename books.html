Recently Completed

Ego is the Enemy

by Ryan Holiday
Completed: August 2025

Ryan Holiday's powerful exploration of how ego can derail success and the importance of humility, continuous learning, and service to something greater than oneself.

Personal Reflections

"Reading 'Ego is the Enemy' was incredibly timely as I navigate my transition from student to professional economist. Holiday's insights on the three stages—aspiring, success, and failure— resonated deeply with my current journey.

As someone involved in research leadership through the Global Strategy Review and various extracurricular roles, the book's emphasis on being a student rather than pretending to be the teacher struck home. It reminded me that despite academic achievements and recognition, the most important quality is remaining humble and focused on continuous learning.

The concept of 'plus est en vous' (there is more in you) particularly inspired me. Working on Lebanon's economic challenges requires looking beyond personal recognition toward serving something greater—the community and the country's economic future. This book reinforced my commitment to using my economics education not for personal ego, but for meaningful impact.

Holiday's message that ego makes us enemies of mastery is especially relevant in economics, where overconfidence can lead to poor policy recommendations. The humility to acknowledge what I don't know while building on what I do know will be crucial as I advance in my career."

Key Takeaways

Always remain a student, never stop learning
Serve something greater than yourself
Success and failure both require humility
Focus on the work, not the recognition

Application to My Work

The principles from this book directly influence how I approach my research with GSR, leadership roles in student organizations, and professional development. It reinforces the importance of staying focused on economic solutions that benefit Lebanon rather than personal academic prestige.

How to Win Friends and Influence People

by Dale Carnegie
Completed: September 2025

Dale Carnegie's timeless classic on human relations and influence. Through practical principles and real-life examples, the book demonstrates how genuine interest in others, empathy, and effective communication create lasting personal and professional relationships.

Personal Reflections

"How to Win Friends and Influence People" provided practical frameworks for the interpersonal aspects of leadership that economics courses don't teach. As someone organizing CEF and coordinating teams across different academic backgrounds for GSR, Carnegie's principles on making people feel important and appreciated have been invaluable.

The principle of "becoming genuinely interested in other people" transformed how I approach networking at conferences like YESF and GFF. Instead of focusing on what I could gain, I started asking delegates about their research and challenges. This shift led to more meaningful connections and collaborative opportunities.

For my role as ESS Logistics Officer organizing the interfaith Ramadan-Lent celebration, Carnegie's insights on respecting others' opinions and avoiding arguments proved essential. Bringing together 200+ Muslims and Christians required sensitivity and the ability to make everyone feel valued - exactly what Carnegie advocates.

The book's emphasis on acknowledging mistakes openly aligns with the humility principle from "Ego is the Enemy." In my volunteer work raising funds for displaced families, being genuine and admitting when approaches weren't working built trust with donors and beneficiaries alike. Carnegie's lessons on influence through empathy rather than force will guide my leadership approach as CEF develops.

Key Takeaways

Become genuinely interested in other people
Make others feel important and appreciated
Listen more, talk less about yourself
Influence through empathy, not force

Application to My Work

Carnegie's principles shape how I coordinate volunteers, negotiate with sponsors, and build relationships with potential CEF speakers. The techniques for winning people to your way of thinking without arousing resentment will be crucial when proposing economic reforms through GSR that challenge entrenched interests.

Poor Economics

by Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo
Completed: October 2025

A groundbreaking examination of poverty through randomized control trials and field experiments. Nobel laureates Banerjee and Duflo challenge conventional wisdom about development economics, showing how small, evidence-based interventions can create meaningful change in poor communities.

Personal Reflections

Reading "Poor Economics" transformed how I think about developmental economics. As someone working on Lebanon's economic challenges through the Global Strategy Review, the book's emphasis on evidence-based policy particularly resonated. Banerjee and Duflo's approach of testing assumptions through controlled experiments offers a powerful alternative to the grand theories that often fail in practice.

The chapter on education challenges the notion that simply building schools solves problems. In Lebanon, where economic crisis affects educational quality, their findings about teacher accountability and targeted interventions provide actionable insights. Their work on microfinance also challenges assumptions I held about financial inclusion strategies.

What struck me most was the focus on understanding how poor people actually make decisions under constraints. This human-centered approach to economics aligns perfectly with my research on sectarian resource allocation and economic development. The book reinforces that effective policy requires understanding local context rather than importing solutions wholesale.

For my work with CEF and GSR, this book provides methodological frameworks for testing interventions before scaling. The randomized control trial approach, while challenging to implement, offers Lebanon a path to evidence-based policymaking that could break the cycle of failed reforms.

Key Takeaways

Test assumptions through rigorous experiments
Understand how poor people actually make decisions
Context matters - local solutions for local problems
Small, targeted interventions can have big impacts

Application to My Work

The evidence-based approach from this book directly influences how I design research for GSR and plan CEF's policy simulation tracks. Testing interventions on small scales before implementing broadly could help Lebanon avoid costly policy mistakes.
